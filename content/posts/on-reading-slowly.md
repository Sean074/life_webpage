I used to read fast. I'd race through a book and feel good about finishing it. Then I'd try to recall something from it a week later and come up with almost nothing. The habit was optimized for the wrong metric.

## The Problem With Speed

Reading fast is fine for news or documentation or anything where you're looking for a specific thing. It's not fine for books you care about. The comprehension is shallow and the retention is poor. You get the rough shape of the argument but lose the texture.

## Slowing Down

The change I made was simple: I read one book at a time and I stop when something lands. Not to highlight it — highlights are a false sense of retention — but to sit with it. Sometimes for a few minutes. Sometimes long enough that I put the book down and don't pick it up again that day.

This sounds inefficient. It is, in terms of books-per-year. But I can tell you what I read last year. I can tell you the argument Simone Weil makes about attention, or what Shetty and Susskind think about the future of the professions. That feels worth something.

## What I'm Reading Now

Mostly history and philosophy. The occasional novel. Technical books get skimmed — there's no shame in that, it's the right tool for the job. But the books I'm reading to think, I read slowly, with a pencil.
